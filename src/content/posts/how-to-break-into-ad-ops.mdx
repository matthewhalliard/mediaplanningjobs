An ad operations career offers one of the clearest technical entry points into digital advertising. Unlike media planning or buying, where relationship skills and strategic thinking dominate early training, ad ops rewards technical aptitude and process orientation from day one. The 2026 market for entry-level ad ops is tight but navigable if you know what to study, where to apply, and what compensation to expect.

This guide covers the tactical steps to break into [ad operations](/jobs/function/ad-operations/): the platforms hiring managers expect you to know, the employers most likely to hire junior talent, and realistic salary benchmarks for your first two years.

## What to study before you apply

Google Ad Manager (GAM) is the industry-standard ad server. If you don't know GAM, you're not competitive for most ad ops roles. Publishers use it to manage direct-sold and programmatic inventory. Agencies use it to traffic campaigns for publisher clients. Brands rarely touch it directly, but in-house ad ops teams at larger companies often work alongside it for verification and troubleshooting.

Google offers free GAM training through Skillshop. Complete the "Google Ad Manager Fundamentals" and "Video Fundamentals" courses. The certification itself doesn't carry much weight, but the knowledge does. Hiring managers will ask you to explain line item priority, key-value targeting, and how programmatic demand competes with direct-sold inventory. If you can't answer those questions, you won't make it past the recruiter screen.

Google Tag Manager (GTM) is the second required tool. GTM manages tracking tags, conversion pixels, and third-party scripts across websites. Ad ops coordinators spend significant time implementing tags, troubleshooting misfires, and auditing tag load times. Publishers expect you to understand triggers, variables, and the data layer. Agencies expect you to coordinate tag implementations with clients' dev teams.

GTM's learning curve is gentler than GAM's. Google's Skillshop course takes four to six hours. Practice by setting up GTM on a personal website or blog. Implement Google Analytics 4, a LinkedIn Insight Tag, and a Meta Pixel. Document the process. Hiring managers ask behavioral questions about tag troubleshooting, and a real example beats hypotheticals.

Basic SQL separates you from other entry-level candidates. Ad ops roles increasingly require pulling reports from data warehouses, analyzing discrepancies between platforms, and building custom audience segments. You don't need to write complex joins or optimize query performance, but you should understand SELECT, WHERE, GROUP BY, and COUNT.

Codecademy's "Learn SQL" course covers the basics in 10-12 hours. Mode Analytics offers a free SQL tutorial focused on marketing and analytics use cases. Practice writing queries against sample datasets. When you interview, mention SQL in your skills section and be ready to explain how you'd use it to reconcile impression discrepancies between GAM and a DSP.

Understanding of the programmatic ecosystem helps but isn't mandatory for entry-level roles. Know the difference between SSPs and DSPs. Understand what header bidding does. Recognize the names Trade Desk, Xandr, Magnite, and PubMatic. You don't need hands-on experience with these platforms to land a coordinator role, but you should be able to hold a conversation about how programmatic transactions work.

## Where to apply: publisher-side first

Publisher-side ad ops is the easiest entry point. Publishers like Condé Nast, Dotdash Meredith, BuzzFeed, Vox Media, and Hearst hire ad ops coordinators with minimal experience. These roles focus on campaign trafficking, line item setup, creative QA, and reporting. The work is repetitive but teaches the fundamentals.

Publishers prefer hiring junior talent because they can train you on their specific GAM setup and internal processes. Turnover is moderate, so openings appear regularly. Salaries run $45K-$55K in major markets, lower in secondary cities. Benefits are standard. Work-life balance depends on the publisher; endemic publishers (food, travel, lifestyle) tend to be calmer than news organizations with 24/7 cycles.

Check publishers' career pages directly. Most don't post every opening on Indeed or LinkedIn. Condé Nast lists roles at `condenast.com/careers`. Dotdash Meredith uses Workday. BuzzFeed posts on Greenhouse. Set up alerts for "ad operations coordinator," "ad ops coordinator," and "campaign coordinator."

Agency-side ad ops is harder to break into without prior experience. Agencies like Horizon Media, Tinuiti, and Merkle prefer to promote from within or hire from other agencies. When they do hire externally for junior roles, they expect polish and some exposure to client work. Salaries start slightly higher—$50K-$60K—but the pressure is greater and hours longer.

If you're targeting agencies, apply to mid-sized shops rather than holding companies. GroupM, Publicis, and IPG fill junior ad ops roles through internal rotations or internships. Independent agencies and specialized digital shops like Wpromote, 360i (now Dentsu Creative), or Basis Technologies are more open to external hires with strong technical skills and no agency baggage.

Brand-side ad ops roles pay the best at entry level—$55K-$65K—but rarely hire true beginners. Retailers like Target, Walmart, and Best Buy have in-house ad ops teams managing retail media networks. CPG brands like Unilever and P&G have small ad ops functions supporting programmatic buys. Streaming platforms like Roku, Paramount, and NBCUniversal need ad ops support for CTV campaigns.

These roles expect one to two years of experience, usually from a publisher or agency. Apply anyway if the job description doesn't explicitly say "2+ years required," but temper expectations. Use these postings to understand what skills employers value, then build those skills on the publisher side first.

## Internships and contract roles as a wedge

Internships in ad ops are less common than in [media planning](/jobs/function/media-planner/) or buying, but they exist. Publishers and agencies occasionally offer summer or semester-long programs. These roles pay $15-$20/hour and focus on campaign setup, creative QA, and reporting. Treat them seriously; many convert to full-time coordinator roles.

Contract roles through staffing agencies like Aquent, Creative Circle, or The Adesis Group are another entry point. These roles last three to six months, pay $20-$28/hour depending on market, and offer no benefits. The work is identical to full-time coordinator roles. Contractors often convert to full-time employees if they perform well and a headcount opens.

Contract-to-hire is common in ad ops. Publishers and agencies use it to evaluate technical skills and cultural fit without committing to a full-time hire. Accept these roles if you're struggling to land a direct hire. Six months of contract experience makes you competitive for the next full-time opening.

## What salary to expect (and negotiate)

Entry-level ad ops coordinator salaries in 2026 range from $45K to $65K depending on employer type, market, and your leverage. Publishers in New York, Los Angeles, and San Francisco start coordinators at $50K-$55K. Chicago, Atlanta, and Boston run $45K-$50K. Remote-first publishers pay toward the lower end unless you're in a high-cost market.

Agencies pay $50K-$60K for junior ad ops roles in major markets. Holding company agencies (GroupM, Publicis, IPG) pay closer to $55K-$60K but expect longer hours. Independent agencies pay $50K-$55K with better work-life balance. Remote agency roles pay $48K-$55K.

In-house brand roles start at $55K-$65K, but again, these rarely hire true entry-level candidates. If you land one, you're already ahead.

Negotiate if you have competing offers or relevant skills. An extra $3K-$5K is realistic if you can point to SQL proficiency, prior internship experience, or a competing offer. Don't negotiate on your first job if you have no leverage; take the offer, learn the systems, and switch employers in 18-24 months for a 15-20% raise.

Benefits matter as much as base salary. Publishers and large agencies offer solid health insurance, 401(k) matching, and PTO. Startups and small digital shops often skimp on benefits. Calculate total compensation before accepting.

## The first 12-18 months: what you'll actually do

Entry-level ad ops is process-heavy and detail-oriented. You'll spend most of your time trafficking campaigns in GAM: creating line items, uploading creatives, setting targeting parameters, and running pre-launch QA. You'll implement tracking tags in GTM, troubleshoot pixel fires, and pull delivery reports.

Expect repetitive work. You'll build dozens of line items per week, many nearly identical. You'll chase down creative assets from account teams. You'll respond to "why is this campaign underdelivering?" Slack messages. You'll join status calls where you're the only person who understands why a VPAID creative won't render on mobile Safari.

This phase teaches the fundamentals. You'll learn how ad serving works, how programmatic demand integrates with direct-sold inventory, and how small setup errors cascade into big delivery problems. You'll become fluent in ad ops terminology. You'll build relationships with account managers, yield teams, and engineers.

After 12-18 months, you'll move into troubleshooting and optimization. You'll take on more complex campaigns: roadblocks, sponsorships, custom audience segments, and programmatic guaranteed deals. You'll start participating in yield optimization discussions. You'll train newer coordinators.

At this point, you're ready for a promotion to ad ops specialist or a lateral move to another employer at a higher level. Publishers promote internally but slowly. Agencies promote faster but expect you to take on account management responsibilities. Brands pay the most but hire cautiously.

## Moving up: specialist to senior in four to six years

The path from entry-level coordinator to senior ad ops takes four to six years if you're intentional about skill-building and employer switches. Coordinators make $45K-$60K. Specialists make $65K-$80K. Senior ad ops roles pay $85K-$110K depending on market and employer.

Promotions within one employer are slow. Publishers promote coordinators to specialists after two to three years. Agencies move faster but cap upside. The fastest path to senior compensation is switching employers every two to three years, each time negotiating a 15-25% raise.

Build technical depth to differentiate yourself. Learn programmatic mechanics: how header bidding auctions work, how SSPs prioritize demand, how to read bid stream data. Pick up Python or R for reporting automation. Get comfortable with Looker, Tableau, or Power BI. Senior ad ops roles increasingly require data analysis and workflow automation, not just campaign trafficking.

Specialize in a high-demand area. CTV ad ops is growing faster than display. Retail media networks (Amazon DSP, Walmart Connect, Kroger Precision Marketing) need ad ops professionals who understand both publisher and advertiser workflows. Programmatic guaranteed and private marketplace deals require deeper technical knowledge than open auction buys.

Publishers, agencies, and brands all hire senior ad ops professionals, but the work differs. Publisher-side senior ad ops focuses on yield optimization, header bidding strategy, and ad tech integrations. Agency-side focuses on campaign troubleshooting, client reporting, and coordinating across DSPs and verification vendors. Brand-side focuses on in-housing programmatic capabilities and managing retail media partnerships.

Your first job is a stepping stone. Take the offer that teaches you GAM and GTM, even if the salary is lower than you want. Build skills, document your work, and switch employers strategically. An ad operations career pays well, offers clear advancement, and insulates you from the AI-driven automation hitting [media planning](/jobs/function/media-planner/) and [buying](/jobs/function/media-buyer/) roles. The work is technical, the demand is steady, and the entry path is clearer than most advertising disciplines.

## FAQs

**What skills do I need to get into ad ops?**

Google Ad Manager and Google Tag Manager are table stakes. Basic SQL helps you stand out. Understanding of ad serving, trafficking, and third-party tag implementation gets you in the door. Publishers often hire for these skills before agencies do.

**Is publisher-side or agency-side easier for entry-level ad ops?**

Publisher-side is significantly easier to break into. Publishers like Condé Nast, Dotdash Meredith, and BuzzFeed hire coordinators with less experience. Agencies expect more polish and often fill junior roles with internal transfers.

**What salary should I expect for entry-level ad ops jobs?**

Entry-level ad ops coordinators at publishers start around $45K-$55K. Agency junior ad ops roles run $50K-$60K. In-house brand roles start higher at $55K-$65K but are harder to land without experience.

**Do I need a degree to work in ad operations?**

Not strictly, but most employers expect a bachelor's degree. Marketing, communications, or business are common, but computer science, data analytics, or even unrelated degrees work if you can demonstrate technical aptitude and self-taught ad tech skills.

**How long does it take to move from entry-level to senior ad ops?**

Two to three years at the coordinator level, then two to three years as a specialist or manager. You can hit senior ad ops ($85K-$110K) in four to six years if you're strategic about building technical depth and switching employers when necessary.
