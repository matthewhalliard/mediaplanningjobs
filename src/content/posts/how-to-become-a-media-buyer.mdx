Media buying in 2026 is more technical and data-driven than ever. Cookie deprecation finally landed in Q1, retail media networks are the fastest-growing channel, and AI-assisted planning is automating the repetitive work that used to fill an assistant's day. That means entry-level media buyers need to prove platform fluency and analytical thinking before they land their first role.

Here's the tactical playbook: the degree paths that matter, the certifications hiring managers actually check, how to build a portfolio when you have zero client experience, and where to target your first application.

## The degree paths that actually help

Most media buyers have a bachelor's degree in marketing, advertising, communications, business, or a related field. You don't need a specific major, but you do need to demonstrate two things: basic quantitative literacy and an understanding of how digital advertising works.

Marketing and advertising programs give you the industry vocabulary and foundational strategy coursework. Business and economics degrees prove you can work with budgets, spreadsheets, and ROI math. Data analytics, statistics, and even psychology majors can make the jump if they pair coursework with the right certifications.

Liberal arts grads get hired, but you'll need to compensate with stronger technical proof points. That means completing every certification below and building a portfolio that shows you understand campaign structure, audience targeting, and performance metrics.

If you're still in school, take classes in statistics, consumer behavior, digital marketing, and data analysis. Minor in data science or business analytics if your school offers it. If you're a career-changer, your degree matters less than your ability to talk fluently about CPMs, conversion funnels, and attribution models in an interview.

## The certifications you need before your first interview

Certifications are table stakes for [entry-level media buyer](/jobs/function/media-buyer/) roles in 2026. Hiring managers expect you to show up with at least three platform certifications completed. They're free, they take 15-30 hours total, and they prove you've touched the tools you'll use daily.

**Google Ads certification** is non-negotiable. Complete Search, Display, and Video modules at minimum. Video is especially critical now that YouTube is the second-largest programmatic video inventory source after CTV exchanges. Hiring managers filter résumés by this certification.

**Meta Blueprint certification** covers Facebook and Instagram buying. Complete the Media Buying Professional track, which includes campaign setup, audience targeting, creative best practices, and Advantage+ automation. Retail and DTC brands run 30-50% of their budgets through Meta, so fluency here is expected.

**The Trade Desk Edge Academy** is the platform certification that separates candidates in 2026. Trade Desk powers programmatic for most independent agencies and many holding company divisions. The free Edge Academy curriculum covers DSP fundamentals, data strategy, and CTV buying. Finish the full track and add "Trade Desk certified" to your LinkedIn headline.

Optional but valuable: **Google Analytics 4 certification** (proves you understand conversion tracking and attribution), **Amazon Advertising certification** (essential if you're targeting retail clients or CPG brands), and **TikTok for Business certification** (smaller but growing fast, especially for Gen Z-focused brands).

Do not pay for third-party "media buying bootcamps" or certificate programs. The vendor certifications above are free, recognized by hiring managers, and teach you the actual platforms you'll use.

## Build a portfolio when you have zero clients

The hardest part of breaking into media buying is proving you can do the work before anyone pays you to do it. Certifications show you passed the quizzes. A portfolio shows you can structure a real campaign.

Create 2-3 mock media plans for real brands. Pick a DTC brand, a local business, and a mobile app. Write a one-page brief for each: business objective, target audience, channel mix, budget allocation, and success metrics. Then build out the tactical layer: recommended ad formats, audience segments, bidding strategies, and a 30-day pacing calendar.

Use real data. Pull audience size estimates from Meta Ads Manager and Google Ads Keyword Planner. Reference CPM benchmarks from industry reports (eMarketer, Advertiser Perceptions, IAB). Show your math: if the client has a $10K monthly budget and you're recommending a 60/40 split between Meta and Google, break down how you'd allocate daily budgets and what performance you'd expect in week one vs. week four.

If you can get real freelance or volunteer work, take it. Offer to run a $500 Meta campaign for a local nonprofit or a friend's Shopify store. Document setup, creative testing, results, and what you learned. Real campaign screenshots—even at tiny budgets—carry more weight than mock plans.

Organize everything in a simple PDF portfolio or a Google Drive folder. Lead with results (even from mock plans: "Projected 4.2x ROAS based on category benchmarks"). Include screenshots of campaign setup, audience builders, and budget pacing tools. Keep it under 10 pages total.

## Where to target your first job

The fastest path into media buying is through an agency assistant or coordinator role, then promoting to buyer within 6-12 months. Most [media buyer career paths](/jobs/function/media-buyer/) start here.

**Holding company agencies** (GroupM, Publicis, Omnicom, IPG, Dentsu) hire large coordinator classes and have structured promotion timelines. You'll start as an assistant media buyer or media coordinator, supporting a team of buyers on a single account. Expect $42K-$50K in secondary markets, $50K-$58K in New York or Chicago. Promotion to media buyer typically happens after one year if you hit performance benchmarks.

**Independent agencies** (Tinuiti, Wpromote, PMG, Horizon Media) hire smaller classes but promote faster. You'll touch more clients and get hands-on platform access earlier. Pay is comparable to holding companies but with less formal training. Better for self-starters who learn by doing.

**Performance marketing agencies** and **digital-first shops** often hire entry-level buyers with no prior experience if you have strong certifications and a portfolio. These agencies focus on paid search, paid social, and programmatic. Expect to manage campaigns within your first 90 days. Pay ranges $48K-$60K depending on market and agency size.

**In-house brand roles** are harder to land as your first job but not impossible. Retail brands, DTC companies, and mobile app publishers sometimes hire junior media buyers with strong technical skills and no agency background. Target brands with 10-50 person marketing teams—they're more likely to hire and train entry-level talent. Starting pay is $55K-$68K but with slower career progression than agencies.

Avoid "media buying internships" that are really data entry or reporting roles with no platform access. If the job description doesn't mention campaign setup, budget management, or optimization, keep looking.

## The skills that get you promoted from assistant to buyer

Once you land the assistant or coordinator role, your goal is to prove you can manage a campaign independently. That means moving beyond task execution (trafficking tags, pulling reports) to strategic optimization (reallocating budget, testing new audiences, recommending creative changes).

Focus on becoming the go-to person for one platform. If your team runs a lot of Trade Desk campaigns, own the CTV troubleshooting and deal ID setup. If you support paid social, master Facebook's Advantage+ automation and TikTok's Spark Ads format. Specialists get promoted faster than generalists at the junior level.

Learn to tell the "why" story behind performance swings. Don't just report that CPMs went up 18% last week—explain that it's Q4 retail competition and recommend shifting 20% of budget to lower-cost dayparts or testing Reels placement. Buyers make recommendations. Assistants report numbers.

Build relationships with platform reps. Google, Meta, and Trade Desk all assign agency support teams. Get on their calendars, ask for beta access to new features, request account audits. Reps remember buyers who ask smart questions and will fast-track your learning.

Track your wins. Keep a running doc of optimizations you recommended, tests you ran, and results you drove. When promotion season comes, you'll have a one-pager of proof points showing you're ready to manage accounts solo.

## What the media buyer career path looks like after year one

Most buyers follow a three-stage path: assistant/coordinator (0-12 months), media buyer (1-3 years), senior media buyer (3-5 years). After senior buyer, you fork into specialist tracks (programmatic, paid social, search) or management (media supervisor, media director).

Total comp for a media buyer with 1-2 years of experience ranges $58K-$72K at agencies, $68K-$80K in-house. Senior buyers with 3-5 years earn $75K-$95K at agencies, $85K-$105K at brands. Programmatic specialists and [ad operations](/jobs/function/ad-operations/) buyers with technical depth can command the higher end of those ranges.

Promotion timelines are faster at independent agencies and in-house teams, slower at holding companies with structured career ladders. If you're not promoted from assistant to buyer within 18 months, start interviewing elsewhere.

The biggest skill gap separating good buyers from great ones in 2026 is understanding first-party data strategy and retail media integration. Cookie deprecation means campaigns now rely on authenticated user data, retail media network audiences (Amazon, Walmart Connect, Kroger Precision Marketing), and probabilistic identity graphs. Buyers who understand how to activate first-party segments across walled gardens and open web programmatic will move up fastest.

## FAQs

**Do I need a specific degree to become a media buyer?**

No specific degree is required, but most entry-level media buyers have degrees in marketing, advertising, communications, business, or data analytics. Some agencies hire liberal arts grads who demonstrate quantitative aptitude and complete relevant certifications before applying.

**What certifications should I get before applying for media buyer jobs?**

Google Ads certification, Meta Blueprint certification, and The Trade Desk Edge Academy are the three most valuable. Complete all three before your first interview. Google Analytics 4 and Amazon Advertising certifications are strong additions for retail-focused roles.

**How long does it take to become a media buyer?**

With a relevant degree and certifications, you can land an entry-level media buyer role immediately after graduation. Career-changers typically need 2-4 months to complete certifications and build a portfolio before interviewing. Expect 6-12 months in an assistant or coordinator role before being promoted to media buyer.

**What's the starting salary for an entry-level media buyer?**

Entry-level media buyers at agencies typically earn $45K-$55K in secondary markets and $52K-$62K in major metros like New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago. In-house brand roles start slightly higher, around $55K-$65K, but are harder to land without agency experience.

**Can I become a media buyer without agency experience?**

Yes, but it's harder. Most entry-level media buyer openings prefer 6-12 months of agency coordinator or assistant experience. Alternatives: complete multiple certifications, build a portfolio with mock campaigns or freelance work, or target small agencies and performance marketing shops that hire less experienced candidates.
